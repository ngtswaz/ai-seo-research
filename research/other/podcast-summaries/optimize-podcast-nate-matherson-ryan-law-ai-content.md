## Episode: Ryan Law on AI Content Engineering
**URL:** https://www.positional.com/podcast/ryan-law-ahrefs
**Approx. date:** Q4 2024

### Key moments

**On the "content engineering" label:**
Ryan explains why calling it content engineering rather than AI writing matters:
> "The moment you call it engineering, you start thinking about systems, inputs, quality gates. The moment you call it writing with AI, you think about prompts and output quality. Engineering is the right mental model."

**On what AI is actually bad at:**
- Generating first-person experience (it hallucinates plausibly, which is worse than obviously wrong)
- Knowing what your company's internal data says
- Understanding your specific customer's vocabulary and pain points
- Making editorial judgment calls ("should we cover this angle or that one?")

**On the Ahrefs workflow:**
Ryan outlines the same process documented in the YouTube transcript (`research/youtube-transcripts/ryan-law/`), with additional detail on the editing stage:
- The brief is the most important document — AI output quality is 80% determined by brief quality
- Section-by-section review is significantly better than reviewing a full draft
- The human rewrites the introduction every time — AI introductions are universally weak

**On measuring success:**
Ahrefs tracks AI-assisted articles in a separate portfolio in their own tool. At time of recording: comparable ranking performance to human-written articles for well-briefed topics, underperformance on niche/emerging topics.

## Episode: Bernard Huang on Content Grading at Scale
**URL:** https://www.positional.com/podcast/bernard-huang-clearscope
**Approx. date:** Q1 2025

### Key moments

**On where content grading fits:**
Bernard argues content grading should happen at the brief stage, not post-publish:
> "If you're grading a finished article and it scores 45, you're going to rewrite half of it. That's more expensive than getting the brief right and grading the outline."

**On AI content and content grades:**
Data from Clearscope's platform: AI-generated content on its first draft typically scores 55–65/100. Human-written content typically scores 60–70/100. After one round of human editing (depth-focused, not grammar), AI content reaches 75–85/100 — above the average human-written piece.

**The insight:** AI content + focused human editing outperforms unedited human content on content grade metrics. The variable is the editing standard.

**On what doesn't move the needle:**
- Word count (diminishing returns above 1,200 words for most topics)
- Exact keyword density (completely irrelevant in 2025)
- Meta description optimisation (marginal)

What does move the needle:
- NLP term coverage (largest single factor)
- Heading structure matching top-performer patterns
- Internal link depth to supporting content
